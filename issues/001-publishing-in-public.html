I'm Publishing in Public So I Have to Finish Things

Field notes from a studio that ships software and takes markdowns.

BY KEVIN BIBELHAUSEN · 2026-05-04

I started Markdown because I was tired of finishing things in private.

Here is what "finishing in private" looked like: I'd ship something — a feature, a system, a decision — and then move on. No record of what worked. No record of what didn't. The knowledge lived in a memory file or a session transcript or just in my head, and a month later I'd have to reconstruct it from scratch because I couldn't find the original reasoning.

That's expensive. It's expensive in time, and it's expensive in the same mistakes twice.

The second reason is simpler: I want to find other operators. Not conference-circuit operators. Not people who talk about operations. People who actually run things — ERP migrations at 2 AM, warehouse software that has to survive the holiday peak, CI/CD pipelines that eat your weekend when they break. That world is real, and the people in it are surprisingly hard to find online. Most of what exists is polished case studies written after the fact, when the pain is safely behind you and everything makes narrative sense.

This isn't that.

Studio b. runs real operations. Heritage Fabrics — a mid-market textile distributor — has been running on our own software stack for about two years. Bolt, our continuous-goods WMS, tracks their inventory in yards and kilograms, not just units. AcuOps handles their Acumatica deployments. Both products started as internal tooling. Both are now sold externally.

The newsletter is downstream of that work. When I write here, I'm writing about what's actually happening — not a reconstruction, not a retrospective, not a pitch. The architectural decisions we're in the middle of, the vendor choices we made last quarter that are either holding or falling apart, the things I'd do differently if I were starting from zero today.

The newsletter is downstream of the work. If the work stops being interesting, the newsletter stops being honest.

That's the constraint I'm choosing to publish under.

Here is a thing that just happened.

Bolt has a feature called ATP write-back. The short version: nightly, Bolt re-evaluates ship dates for every open sales order at Heritage Fabrics, then writes the corrected dates back into Acumatica. It automates math that Sarah, our ops lead, was doing by hand.

We shipped ATP write-back in late April. Every metric said it was working. CI green. Deployment successful. I noted it in the project file as live.

It wasn't working. It had been failing on every single write for two weeks.

3,597 failed write attempts. Zero successes. The error log was there the whole time — buried in a Railway service that I wasn't watching because there was no reason to watch a feature that had "shipped."

The feature passed every test we ran. The tests were asking the wrong question.

Here is what actually happened. A separate workstream — extending Bolt's Acumatica integration to a custom endpoint called StudioB — added an environment variable to Railway: ACUMATICA_ENDPOINT=StudioB. That endpoint only knows about a handful of custom sales order fields. It knows nothing about purchase orders, inventory, or most of the other entities Bolt touches.

The problem was in a factory function: createAcumaticaClient(). I had written it to fall back to process.env.ACUMATICA_ENDPOINT if no explicit endpoint was passed. It seemed convenient at the time. It meant the one workstream that needed the StudioB endpoint could just set an env var and move on.

What it actually meant was that every other callsite — ATP write-back, PO sync, inventory sync, the CS order entry tool — silently picked up StudioB instead of Default. Every Acumatica operation that didn't work on the custom endpoint started returning 404s. Quietly. With no alarm.

What caught it wasn't monitoring. It was a new page I'd been building for the imports dashboard — a full-page view of in-flight purchase orders. When I loaded that view for the first time, it was empty. Every PO route was returning nothing. That was impossible, so I started digging. Two hours later I'd traced it back to the env var.

Six PRs to fix it. I also deleted the env var fallback from the factory entirely. The new pattern: explicit endpoint or a hardcoded safe default. No opportunistic env reads.

The guard now lives in the codebase, not in my memory.

The lesson I'm encoding: if a feature is "live," that means nothing until you have observable evidence that it's actually running. Success-event count. Row count in the right table. Something in a log that a human or an alert is actually reading. Schema-verified and CI-green is a starting point, not a finish line.

A note on what this publication is and isn't.

Markdown is my publication, not Studio b.'s firm blog. The firm is Studio b. This is where I think in public on its surface.

I write it when there's something to say. The subscribe form on the homepage says // issued when there's something to say, and that's accurate — I don't have a cadence, I have a standard. If it isn't worth writing, I won't write it. If it is, you'll get it.

What you'll consistently find here: things that are still in motion, technical decisions with actual trade-offs named, vendor calls where I name the vendor, and occasionally something that turned out to be wrong.

What you won't find: polished post-mortems designed to make the failure look like it was always part of the plan, warm-up content that exists to hit a publishing schedule, or anything that could appear verbatim on a generic SaaS blog.

The inline // issued when there's something to say is the whole editorial policy. I don't have a better way to say it.

If you're reading this and you actually run things — ERP, warehouse ops, technical ops at a mid-market distributor, a product team building tooling your own company uses — I'd like to know what you're working on.
